## Profile Specs

### Conservative (Default for Most Runners)

- **Best for**: Beginners, injury-prone runners, those building base, runners over 40, returning from injury
- **Intensity split**: 85% easy / 15% hard; true beginners start 90–100% easy for first 2–3 months
- **Workout frequency**: 1 quality session per week
- **Strides**: 2x per week starting week 2–3
- **Target ACWR**: 0.8–1.2
- **Weekly progression**: 5–10% max during build weeks
- **Deload**: Every 3–4 weeks (30–40% volume reduction from peak); 3 weeks up, 1 week down
- **Speed work introduction**: Week 4–6 for intermediate; week 8–12 for true beginners
- **Philosophy**: Safety first — arrive healthy at the start line

### Moderate (Balanced Approach)

- **Best for**: Intermediate runners (1–3 years), injury-free with solid base, general fitness goals
- **Intensity split**: 80% easy / 20% hard (true 80/20 rule)
- **Workout frequency**: 1–2 quality sessions per week (build from 1 to 2 over 4–6 weeks)
- **Strides**: 3x per week starting week 1–2
- **Micro-intervals**: Added to easy runs for pace variation
- **Target ACWR**: 0.8–1.3
- **Weekly progression**: 10–15% max during build weeks (not every week)
- **Deload**: Every 3 weeks (30% volume reduction from peak); 3 weeks up, 1 week down
- **Recovery**: Minimum 24 hours between quality sessions
- **Speed work introduction**: Week 2–3
- **Philosophy**: Balance performance gains with injury prevention

### Performance-Focused (Speed Priority) ⚠️

- **Best for**: Experienced runners (3+ years), competitive racers, those with strong training history, post-race building to next goal
- **Intensity split**: 75% easy / 25% hard; polarized early season, pyramidal near racing
- **Workout frequency**: 2 quality sessions per week from start
- **Strides**: 3x per week from week 1
- **Target ACWR**: 0.8–1.4; brief controlled spikes to 1.4 acceptable for 1–2 weeks only, then return to 1.3
- **Weekly progression**: 15–20% max during build weeks; 3 weeks up, 1 week down (sometimes 2 up, 1 down if very aggressive)
- **Deload**: Every 3–4 weeks (30% volume reduction)
- **Recovery**: Minimum 48 hours between hard quality sessions
- **Speed work introduction**: Week 1–2 (can start immediately for experienced runners)
- **Periodization**: Polarized (very easy + very hard) in base phase; pyramidal (more threshold) near racing
- **Single-session spike**: No single run >110% of longest run in last 30 days
- **Philosophy**: Maximize speed development; accept higher injury risk
- **Warning**: Significantly higher injury risk. Experienced runners with strong training history only.

## Cross-Profile Rules

These apply to all three profiles:

- Progress only **one variable at a time** — frequency, duration, OR intensity. Changing multiple simultaneously substantially increases injury risk.
- Monitor ACWR weekly to catch spikes before they become injuries.
- Deload weeks are non-negotiable — the body adapts during recovery, not during the hard weeks.
- At least 48 hours between hard workouts.

### Post-Race Scenarios

- **Recovery period**: 1–2 weeks of easy running only at 50–70% of pre-race volume (longer for marathons)
- **Experienced runners (3+ years, no injury history)**: Can start Performance-Focused immediately after the recovery period; they have the chronic load and tissue adaptation. Don't force a conservative base phase on established runners.
- **Intermediate runners or those with recent niggles**: Spend 2–4 weeks re-establishing base before reintroducing workouts

## Weekly Run Distribution

Use this matrix to set up the weekly schedule based on the runner's desired frequency and chosen profile.

### 3 Days/Week (Beginner or Time-Limited)

- 2 easy runs + 1 long run
- **Conservative**: Strides 1–2x/week after week 2–3; one workout after week 4–6
- **Moderate**: Strides 2x/week from week 2; one workout from week 3
- **Performance-Focused**: Strides 2x/week from week 1; one workout from week 1–2

### 4 Days/Week (Intermediate)

- 2 easy runs + 1 workout (tempo or intervals) + 1 long run
- **Conservative**: Strides 2x/week; 1 workout/week from week 4–6
- **Moderate**: Strides 3x/week; 1 workout/week from week 2; build to 2 workouts by mid-plan
- **Performance-Focused**: Strides 3x/week from week 1; 2 workouts/week from week 2

### 5 Days/Week (Intermediate to Advanced)

- 2–3 easy runs (one may be recovery) + 1–2 workouts (different stimuli) + 1 long run
- **Conservative**: Strides 2x/week; 1 workout/week
- **Moderate**: Strides 3x/week; 1–2 workouts/week (vary the stimulus)
- **Performance-Focused**: Strides 3x/week; 2 workouts/week (polarized or pyramidal)

### 6–7 Days/Week (Advanced)

- 3–4 easy/recovery runs + 2 quality sessions + 1 long run; may include double-run days
- **All profiles**: Strides 3x/week; 2 workouts/week minimum

### Workout Distribution Principles

- Hard workouts need at least 48 hours between them (easy day or rest day)
- The long run is typically the longest single effort of the week
- Easy runs make up 75–85% of weekly volume depending on profile
- Recovery runs (6+ days/week) are shorter and easier than regular easy runs
- Pace variation is itself injury prevention — never let every run be the same pace

Since plans don't assign specific days, remind runners to space hard efforts appropriately when scheduling their own week.

## Strides and Micro-Intervals

### Strides

Strides are 20–30 second bursts at 85–90% effort that improve running economy and form with minimal injury risk. Research shows 2% running economy improvement and 3.2% performance gain after just 40 days.

**How to do strides:**
1. Perform after an easy run (not when fatigued from a hard workout)
2. Find a flat, clear section of road or track
3. Gradually accelerate for 20–30 seconds to 85–90% effort (not a full sprint)
4. Focus on smooth, controlled form with quick turnover
5. Walk back to start for full recovery (60–90 seconds)
6. Repeat 4–8 times
7. Total time: 5–10 minutes

Strides are short enough that they don't count toward intensity workload — they're a movement-quality tool, not a fitness stimulus.

**Example cue**: "After your easy run, do 6 strides: 20 seconds at 85–90% effort, walk back for full recovery. Think 'fast but controlled' — not an all-out sprint."

### Micro-Intervals (Moderate Profile)

For pace variation without the fatigue of a full workout, add 30–60 second pickups during easy runs:

- Placed during the middle 3–4 miles of an easy run
- 30–60 seconds at steady/moderate pace (not hard)
- 2–3 minutes easy recovery between
- Repeat 4–6 times
- Purpose: vary the stimulus, reduce repetitive microtrauma from constant pace

## Research Basis

Recent sports science findings (2024–2025) underpinning these profiles:

- **Pace variation prevents overuse injuries**: Running the same pace repeatedly causes repetitive microtrauma. Research suggests 80–90% of running injuries occur suddenly during single sessions, often linked to lack of pace variation.
- **Strides improve economy with low injury risk**: 2% running economy improvement and 3.2% performance gain after just 40 days of stride work.
- **Interval training enhances performance**: 6–7% VO2max improvement over 6 weeks, though injury data remains limited.
- **Polarized training works**: Studies show 30% greater marathon improvement vs. pyramidal approach.
- **Individual response varies**: ~31.5% of runners respond best to polarized, ~31.9% to pyramidal, ~18.7% to both — justifying the profile choice rather than a one-size approach.
- **Single-session spike risk**: Injury risk increases significantly when any single run exceeds 10% of the longest run in the last 30 days. Monitor individual session spikes, not just weekly totals.
- **The 10% weekly rule has limits**: Recent research shows individual variation matters significantly. The "3 weeks up, 1 week down" pattern with experience-appropriate percentages is more effective than a rigid 10% cap.
